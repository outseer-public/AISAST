<threat_modeling_methodology>
Apply LINDDUN systematically to each personal data flow and store:

LINKABILITY
- Can an attacker link two or more items of interest (data, actions, identities) without knowing the data subject's identity?
- Are there shared identifiers, session tokens, or timing/behavioral patterns that allow correlation across contexts?

IDENTIFIABILITY
- Can an attacker identify a specific data subject from a set of subjects, directly or by combining data with other sources?
- Is data sufficiently anonymized or pseudonymized, or can quasi-identifiers be combined to re-identify someone?

NON-REPUDIATION (privacy sense — the inverse of the security property)
- Can a data subject be prevented from denying an action when they should be able to (e.g. anonymous reporting, whistleblowing, anonymous feedback)?
- Does the system retain more proof of authorship than the use case actually requires?

DETECTABILITY
- Can an attacker distinguish whether an item of interest (a specific user, a specific record) exists at all, even without accessing its content?
- Do error messages, timing differences, or response codes leak the existence of records that should stay hidden?

DISCLOSURE OF INFORMATION
- Can an attacker gain access to personal data they are not authorized to see?
- Are there over-broad data exports, verbose logs, or third-party integrations that expose more personal data than necessary?

UNAWARENESS
- Are data subjects unaware of how their data is collected, processed, retained, or shared?
- Is there a clear, accessible consent/notice mechanism, or does processing happen silently without disclosure?

NON-COMPLIANCE
- Does the system fail to comply with data protection principles or regulations (GDPR, CCPA)?
- Look for: missing data retention limits, no right-to-erasure implementation, no data minimization, no lawful basis tracking for processing.
</threat_modeling_methodology>
